', ' \nIn various political initiatives in recent years the Federal Council has been instructed to \naddress the topic of hydraulic fracturing / fracking and to state its position. In his report \n"Fracking in Switzerland", the Federal Council has come to the conclusion that there is no \nreason to ban the technology, and that the existing legal framework at the federal level also \ncovers the technology. However, the Federal Council endorses a uniform regulators practice \nin the cantons, who exert sovereignty over the utilization of the subsurface; a sovereignty \nthat is exerted in a highly variable way. \nThe interdepartmental working group’s report "Fracking in Switzerland" examines \npossible effects of this technology on humans and the environment. The hazards of \nhydraulic fracturing include triggering earthquakes, which might, for example damage \ninfrastructure and buildings. Chemical additives, used for example to inhibit bacterial \ngrowth or decomposition of the fracking fluids, might contaminate ground and surface \nwaters. Potential hazards include naturally occuring subsurface pollutants transported \nfrom large depths to surface, such as heavy metals, hydrocarbons or radioactive \nmaterials, as well as methane gas.  ', '  \nPrinciples defined \nIt is the opinion of the Federal Council that risks to humans and the environment, associated \nwith drilling deep boreholes (with or without hydraulic fracturing) can be reduced to an \nacceptable level if existing environmental regulations are implemented correctly and if the \nprinciples defined in the report are applied (see text box). The Federal Council refrains from a \nmoratorium on fracking, since existing legal regulations and the knowledge in relation to the ', ' 2/3   \ntechnology are adequate for possible projects. However, for reasons of climate policy, the use \nof fracking for the exploitation of fossil natural gas reserves is not supported.  ', ' \nNext steps \nAs a next step, DETEC (the Federal Department of the Environment, Transport, Energy and \nCommunications) will examine whether the Environmental Impact Assessment Ordinance \nand other relevant legal bases (e.g. the Chemical Risk Reduction Ordinance) need to be \namended when applied to drilling deep wells and hydraulic fracturing. Subsequently, the \nfeasibility and need for the establishment of a central register will be examined. Finally, cantons should standardize their regulatory \npractice for deep wells. The DETEC will develop the fundamentals, among them the \nprinciples for safe and environmentally compatible hydraulic fracturing. ', ' \nText box  \nPrinciples for hydraulically fractured, deep wells  \n\uf0b7 Fracking fluids must not contain any persistent substances hazardous to health or \nenvironment. \n\uf0b7 All the substances and components used and their quantities must be declared.  \n\uf0b7 Fluids used must be safely stored, handled and subsequently disposed; likewise \nflowback as well as muds after the hydraulic fracturing.  \n\uf0b7 With regard to the construction, operation and decommissioning of infrastructures, \noperations must be in compliance with stringent safety standards among others, in \nrelation to soil and water contamination and greenhouse gas emissions. \n\uf0b7 Hydraulic fracturing operations are subject to an integral risk management approach \n(covering the entire project life cycle). \n\uf0b7 To adequately analyse risks, knowledge of the subsurface condition must be \nextended, if needed. \n\uf0b7 The ‘polluter pays’ principle applies, with clearly defined responsibilities.
